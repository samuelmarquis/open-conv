## ANGLE: kemp

The paper is confirmed: Michael J. Kemp (Sintefex Audio Lda, S. Marcos da Serra, Portugal), "Analysis and Simulation of Non-Linear Audio Processes using Finite Impulse Responses Derived at Multiple Impulse Amplitudes," AES 106th Convention, Munich, Germany, 8–11 May 1999, Preprint #4919 (session J-5). Sintefex republished the identical text as an app-note titled simply "Dynamic Convolution" (Copyright 1999/2000; obtained in full via Wayback Machine, http://www.sintefex.com/docs/appnotes/dynaconv.PDF). The algorithm: apply 128 linearly-spaced step impulses (from silence up to full scale) to the device under test, first-order-difference each recorded step response to get 128 normalized unit impulse responses h1…h128 (h_M = peak, h1 = lowest level, all normalized to peak amplitude by dividing out fs/(fs−m·d)). At playback, a selector S(x(n)) = 1 + ⌊|x(n)|/(fs/M)⌋ picks the impulse response bracketing each input sample's instantaneous magnitude, and a linear interpolation fraction p(x(n)) = (|x(n)| mod (fs/M))/(fs/M) crossfades between the two adjacent responses, giving y(n) = Σ_k x(n−k)·[p(x(n))·h_S(x(n−k))(k) + (1−p(x(n)))·h_(S(x(n−k))−1)(k)] — i.e., every tap of the convolution is itself a 2-tap linear blend, executed as two MACs per tap (Kemp reports ~100% MAC efficiency on a SHARC DSP, roughly half the throughput of ordinary single-IR convolution). Two extensions are already in the 1999 paper: (a) generalizing the selector from instantaneous |x(n)| to an attack/decay envelope follower (explicitly to avoid switching artifacts) and (b) a second "bilinear" control dimension (e.g. a compressor's gain-reduction state, a user knob, or an LFO) selected via bilinear interpolation across a 2-D grid of impulse-response sets — this is the direct textual ancestor of "modulatable, multi-dimensional IR family selection," relevant to both prongs of the user's project. Kemp built a hardware realization, the Sintefex FX8000 "Replicator" (up to 37 ADSP-21065L SHARC chips at 60 MHz, cascaded 2×4 in stereo for 2048 taps/channel at 50 kHz, a 9th SHARC as sample coordinator, 3 GB internal drive, 24-bit internal / 20-bit+ I/O, expandable to 8 channels/96 kHz) — this is a direct-form time-domain implementation (not FFT-partitioned), because per-sample IR reselection breaks block-FFT convolution's stationarity assumption.

Patent family: the core amplitude-selection/crossfade patent is US 7,039,194 B1, "Audio effects synthesizer with or without analyser" (Kemp; filed 8 Aug 1997 as PCT/GB97/02159, priority UK GB9616755.6, 9 Aug 1996; granted 2 May 2006; also published as related PCT filings) — claims cover measuring impulse responses at multiple discrete impulse amplitudes, selecting/proportionately blending the two adjacent responses per sample, noise-floor splicing/replacement of low-level (noisy) IRs, and an envelope-tracking selection mode. A second, dynamics-specific patent, US 7,095,860 B1 / WO 2000/028521 A1, "Audio dynamic control effects synthesiser with or without analyser" (Kemp; assignee Sintefex Audio Lda on the WO; filed 11 Nov 1999, priority 11 Nov 1998), covers bilinear bracket-interpolation across both signal amplitude and a separately-measured compression-ratio/gain-reduction axis (1 dB steps, −40 to 0 dBm), matching Kemp's companion AES 109th Convention paper, "Analysis and Simulation of Analogue Dynamic Compressors and Limiters in the Digital Domain," AES Preprint 5185 (2000). Commercially, dynamic convolution was licensed by Focusrite for the Liquid Channel (hardware channel strip) and Liquid Mix (plug-in front end to an outboard DSP card, meaning near-zero host-CPU load even with 32 simultaneous channels, per Sound on Sound/Tape Op reviews) — both branded around Sintefex's patented process.

A parallel, competing lineage exists from Angelo Farina's group at the University of Parma, using "diagonal Volterra kernels" derived from a single exponential-sine-sweep measurement (harmonic-order separation) rather than Kemp's discrete-amplitude IR family: Farina, Bellini & Armelloni, "Non-Linear Convolution: A New Approach for the Auralization of Distorting Systems," AES 110th Convention, Amsterdam, 12–15 May 2001 (full text obtained — gives the diagonal-Volterra derivation, the CoolEdit-plugin FFT-partitioned real-time implementation, and a 12-subject A/B listening test showing the synthetic and real distorted recordings were rated "identical" 1.25/5 on average). This was extended in Farina & Armelloni, "Emulation of Not-Linear, Time-Variant Devices by the Convolution Technique" (AES Italian section, Como, Nov 2005), which explicitly names "Impulse Response switching" (Kemp/Sintefex) and "diagonal Volterra multiple convolution" (Farina) as the two known nonlinear-convolution families and evaluates both on memoryless vs. memory-bearing (compressor-like) devices; and again in Farina & Farina, "Real-Time Auralization Employing a Not-Linear, Not-Time-Invariant Convolver" (AES Paper 7295, 2007), which adds real-time morphing/crossfading among kernels for time-variant effects — i.e., an independent line of research converging on exactly the same "crossfade between multiple pre-measured kernels" idea the user's plugin needs. On the CPU-cost side, Primavera, Cecchi, Romoli, Gasparini & Piazza (Università Politecnica delle Marche), "Approximation of Dynamic Convolution Exploiting Principal Component Analysis: Objective and Subjective Quality Evaluation," AES 133rd Convention, San Francisco, Oct 2012 (Paper 8792), plus a companion conference paper "An Efficient DSP Implementation of a Dynamic Convolution Approach Using Principal Component Analysis" (EDERC 2012, DOI 10.1109/EDERC.2012.6532219) directly attack the workload/memory cost of storing and convolving against dozens of full-length IRs by decomposing the amplitude-indexed IR family with PCA/SVD and convolving against a small number of principal-component basis filters weighted by amplitude-dependent coefficients — cutting both memory and MAC count versus naive per-sample dynamic convolution, tested on guitar preamp, distortion and compressor case studies against "existing state-of-the-art techniques." Finally, the most current academic verdict comes from Comunità, Steinmetz & Reiss, "Differentiable Black-box and Gray-box Modeling of Nonlinear Audio Effects" (arXiv:2502.14405, Feb 2025), which explicitly cites Kemp 1999 ([78]) and Primavera et al. 2012 ([79]) as "the main non-differentiable black-box methods…based on Volterra series and dynamic convolution," immediately following its statement that Volterra-based methods are "sufficiently accurate only for weakly nonlinear systems, making it not applicable to most nonlinear audio effects," then states "the same applies to dynamic convolution" — i.e. contemporary DSP/ML literature treats amplitude-indexed dynamic convolution as accuracy-limited for strongly nonlinear, memory-bearing devices, which is exactly the gap the user's proposed engine should target. Commercially, Acustica Audio's Nebula/Acqua line (VVKT — "Vectorial Volterra Kernels Technology") is the most direct modern descendant of both lineages: it samples "diagonal Volterra kernels" per instantaneous snapshot and explicitly supports runtime crossfading between kernels via synth-style modulators (LFOs, envelope followers, "FUNS") — user/developer forum threads (KVR "dynamic volterra kerneling (convolution) plugin," "Real-time dynamic convolution") confirm community awareness that the Kemp/Sintefex patents cover per-sample amplitude-indexed IR blending and discuss it as computationally nontrivial in direct (non-PCA-reduced) form.

## ANGLE: partitioned

Real-time convolution engineering splits into two layers: (A) low/zero-latency engine architecture for long FIR filters, and (B) mechanisms bolted onto that architecture to let the filter itself change over time without clicks.

Layer A is anchored by William G. Gardner, "Efficient Convolution without Input-Output Delay," JAES vol. 43 no. 3, pp. 127-136, March 1995 (AES E-lib elib=7957; also patented as US6574649 "Efficient convolution method and apparatus"). Gardner's hybrid convolver runs the first IR segment as direct time-domain FIR (zero I/O delay) and the remainder as a cascade of FFT block convolutions ("frequency-domain delay lines", FDLs) whose block sizes double geometrically (N, N, 2N, 2N, 4N, 4N, ...), trading a modest excess of multiply-adds for near-O(log N) asymptotic cost at zero latency. This non-uniformly-partitioned (NUP) idea was formalized by Guillermo Garcia, "Optimal Filter Partition for Efficient Convolution with Short Input/Output Delay," 113th AES Convention, Los Angeles, Oct 2002, paper 5660 (AES elib=11275): given a target latency and filter length, a Viterbi search over a convex per-partition cost function finds the cost-minimal partition schedule (uniform, two-stage/double-FDL, or fully general NUP); the search is O(N^2) time / 2N^2 bytes where N=ceil(IR_len/latency) but runs once offline (a clean open-source implementation of exactly this Viterbi search is github.com/michaelkrzyzaniak/Partitioned-Convolution). Frank Wefers' PhD thesis, "Partitioned convolution algorithms for real-time auralization," RWTH Aachen/Logos Verlag Berlin, 2015 (IV+258pp, publications.rwth-aachen.de record 466561), is the most complete modern treatment, generalizing uniform/non-uniform/unpartitioned fast convolution and explicitly covering "exchange of filter impulse responses" as one of its named contributions. Supporting Wefers/Vorländer papers fill in the theory: DAFx-11 "Optimal Filter Partitions for Real-Time FIR Filtering using Uniformly-Partitioned FFT-based Convolution in the Frequency-Domain" (pp.155-161); AES 45th Conference, Helsinki 2012, "Optimal Filter Partitions for Non-Uniformly Partitioned Convolution" (AES elib=16208); AES 133rd Convention, San Francisco 2012, paper 8788, "Potential of non-uniformly partitioned convolution with freely adaptable FFT sizes"; and AES 137th Convention, LA 2014, paper 9205, "Uniformly-partitioned convolution with independent partitions in signal and filter" (decoupling input-block size from filter-partition size inside one FDL bank, directly useful for a Rust engine's threading/SIMD granularity).

Layer B — click-free time-varying IR updates — has three engineering tiers, cheapest to costliest. (1) Wefers & Vorländer first proposed DFT-domain crossfading in "Frequency domain filter exchange for DFT-based fast convolution," AIA-DAGA 2013, Merano (p.263), then expanded it in "Efficient time-varying FIR filtering using crossfading implemented in the DFT domain," Forum Acousticum 2014 (cited as ref [16] in the DAFx-17 paper below): naive time-domain crossfading of two parallel full convolutions doubles FFT/IFFT cost for the transition window; doing the crossfade directly on DFT-domain outputs before the single inverse transform removes the extra IFFT, halving the transition-period overhead relative to the naive double-convolver. (2) Øyvind Brandtsegg & Sigurd Saue, "Live Convolution with Time-Variant Impulse Response," DAFx-17, Edinburgh, pp.239-246 (full text obtained and read) — go further and show that for a uniformly-partitioned FDL convolver, sequential partition-by-partition replacement of the IR (load new partitions into the same slots vacated by outgoing IR partitions, in the same order, starting at a partition boundary n_T=k*N_P) reproduces the exact output of two overlapping full convolutions (measured SNR difference 67.2dB in their listening-signal test, N=44100-sample speech IRs, partition NP=1024, FFT block NB=2048) at *zero added computational cost* — no extra FFT, no crossfade multiply, because the technique exploits partitioned convolution's inherent property that partition x_i * h_j only contributes to output interval Y_k for i,j<=k (loading) and only up to Y_{P+k} for i,j>k (unloading), so the fade-in/fade-out is already "baked into" the convolution sum. The only cost is a fixed transition length equal to the full filter length N (or P-1 partitions) that the user cannot shorten; the paper flags as open work whether this generalizes cleanly to overlap-save (vs. their overlap-add) and to non-uniform partitioning. They implemented it as the Csound `liveconv` opcode (built on the existing uniformly-partitioned `ftconv` opcode) and shipped a "Live convolver" VST built around it; a related/extended journal treatment appears to exist as "Live Convolution with Time-Varying Filters," Applied Sciences 8(1):103, 2018 (MDPI, paywalled to WebFetch — worth pulling PDF directly, likely same NTNU authors). (3) The oldest patented mechanism is US6421697B1 "Variants on low-latency convolution — smooth updating of filter response using crossfades," inventors David S. McGrath & Andrew P. Reilly, assignee Lake DSP Pty Ltd (later Lake Technology, then Dolby Labs), filed 1999/granted 2002: it crossfades the *overlapping output regions* of successive OLA blocks with complementary fade windows when the filter coefficients change between blocks — conceptually the ancestor of the "double-convolver" approach and worth citing as prior art even though (per Wefers/Brandtsegg) it is not the cheapest option. Note: the specific numeric embodiment values I retrieved for this patent (N=128, overlap O=32 etc.) came from an automated fetch summary and should be re-verified against the actual claims/spec before quoting in a spec document — the mechanism description (crossfade of OLA overlap regions via complementary raised-cosine-type windows) is corroborated across multiple independent sources and is solid, but I did not personally confirm the exact sample counts.

Open-source engines worth studying directly: jconvolver (Fons Adriaensen) — JACK NUP convolution engine distributing partition sizes across up to 5 priority-ranked threads; HISSTools Impulse Response Toolbox (Alex Harker, Pierre Alexandre Tremblay, Pete Dowling, U. Huddersfield, presented as "The HISSTools Impulse Response Toolbox: Convolution for the Masses," ICMC 2012) — Max/MSP toolbox whose `multiconvolve~` combines direct time-domain convolution for the IR's early part with FFT-partitioned convolution for the tail, a la Gardner, BSD-licensed on GitHub (HISSTools/HISSTools_Impulse_Response_Toolbox); HiFi-LoFi/FFTConvolver — a small, real-time-safe (no locks/allocations in the audio thread), MIT/permissive C++ convolution library with both a uniform-block `FFTConvolver` and a `TwoStageFFTConvolver` (Gardner-style double-FDL), used inside HiFi-LoFi/KlangFalter (JUCE-based open-source convolution-reverb plugin with stretch/begin-point/attack-length IR parameters, though its README doesn't document click-avoidance for those parameter edits — worth inspecting its source directly since the angle-2 problem (parametric IR edits without clicks) is apparently unsolved even in this mature FOSS project); there is also a Rust port, neodsp/fft-convolver, directly relevant as a starting point or reference for the target Rust DSP engine. For the binaural/auralization side: the 3D Tune-In (3DTI) Toolkit, "3D Tune-In Toolkit: An open-source library for real-time binaural spatialisation," PLOS ONE 14(3):e0211899, 2019 (3DTune-In/3dti_AudioToolkit on GitHub) renders HRTF convolution via standard uniformly-partitioned overlap-save, but obtains click-free continuous IR change under a moving/rotating source by barycentric interpolation of the FIR taps among the three closest measured HRIRs before each convolution block (with ITD handled and interpolated separately from the spectral IR) — an important alternative design pattern to crossfading: continuously blend filter *coefficients* per block rather than blending filter *outputs*, valid whenever the interpolation target is itself smooth (adjacent HRIR positions), which is a good template for e.g. smoothly modulating "damping" or "size" parameters that map to a continuum of pre-computed or parametrically-generated IRs. EVERTims (Poirier-Quinot, Katz et al., "EVERTims: Open source framework for real-time auralization in architectural acoustics and virtual reality," DAFx-17) is a relevant end-to-end moving-source auralization pipeline (Blender scene editor + C++ image-source/ray-tracing acoustic solver + JUCE auralization engine, image-source model for early reflections + FDN for late reverberation) though its convolution-update strategy specifically wasn't detailed in what I could retrieve. Also flag Müller-Tomfelde, "Time-Varying Filter in Non-Uniform Block Convolution," DAFx-2001, as an early (pre-Wefers) treatment specifically of time-varying filtering inside a non-uniform block-convolution engine — good historical context/citation even though I could not pull full text.

For the Rust engine design implication: the cheapest and most robust default should be partition-wise incremental replacement (Brandtsegg & Saue) layered on top of a Garcia/Wefers-optimal NUP/FDL bank, since it adds zero steady-state CPU cost and only fixes a transition length; DFT-domain crossfading (Wefers/Vorländer) or full double-buffer crossfade (McGrath & Reilly / classic "double convolver") should be reserved as a fallback specifically for changes that alter partition *topology* itself (e.g., a stretch/time-scale parameter that changes total IR length, forcing different NUP schedules) where partition-slot replacement doesn't apply cleanly; and continuous-parameter interpolation-of-taps (3DTI-style) is the right model whenever "damping"/"size" are continuous scalars driving a smoothly parametrized IR family rather than a discrete swap between two arbitrary IRs.

## ANGLE: timevarying

Time-varying FIR theory converges on one diagnosis: convolution is only "safe" (click-free) while the kernel h(n) is constant across the analysis window used to compute the output. The moment h(n) changes, existing fast-convolution engines break in one of two ways depending on architecture. In direct/OLA/OLS block schemes, an abrupt coefficient swap creates a genuine sample-domain discontinuity because the running weighted sum switches which weights it uses instantaneously. In frequency-domain block schemes it is worse: since each output block is produced by a circular multiplication that assumes H is constant over the whole FFT frame, a swap mid-block either leaves stale overlap-remainder samples computed with the old filter (OLA: empirically shown by Jaeger et al. 2023 to cause a burst of broadband distortion lasting roughly one impulse-response-length before settling) or produces a hard first-order discontinuity at the block boundary (OLS: mathematically "clean" per-block but audibly a click). Both amount to a step discontinuity propagating through the system, and by basic Fourier theory a time-domain step smears energy broadband — the perceptual "spectral splatter"/clicking every one of these papers is fighting.

The literature converges on a handful of named mitigation families with concrete, comparable latency/CPU numbers. (1) Output crossfading — run old-IR and new-IR convolutions in parallel and blend the two output streams with a fade window — is the technique patented outright by Lake DSP (McGrath & Reilly, US 6,421,697 B1, filed 1999/granted 2002): overlap-save convolution with an explicit W(n)=0.5+0.5cos(π(n+1)/(O+1)) fade-up/fade-down applied to overlapping output blocks, built to kill "distracting audible clicks" in head-tracked binaural rendering. Its cost is ~2x compute during the transition (two parallel LTI convolutions), which Wefers & Vorländer (Forum Acousticum 2014) address by moving the crossfade into the DFT domain: because a sin²/cos² fade envelope's DFT has only 3 nonzero coefficients, the crossfade becomes a 3-point spectral convolution, saving a full IFFT per transition versus doing it in the time domain (this paper is cited as the state of the art by nearly every later paper I found, including DAFx17 and the 2016 "Code & Sound" implementation writeup). (2) Input/partition commutation — instead of crossfading two output streams, exploit convolution's own inherent "ramp in/ramp out" property (a filter's contribution to output y(n) only spans a bounded interval in partitioned convolution) to swap IR partitions in place, with zero added multiply-add cost. This is exactly Brandtsegg & Saue's "stepwise IR replacement" (DAFx17, NTNU): they formally prove which input/IR partition pairs contribute to which output interval (their Properties 1 & 2), then simply load new IR partitions into the same buffer used by the running convolution; measured SNR between this and a reference "two overlapped time-invariant convolutions" ground truth was 67.2 dB, i.e., audibly indistinguishable, at literally zero extra multiply cost — the price is that the transition duration is fixed at the full filter length N (not user-adjustable) and every parameter change requires refilling the whole N-sample/partition IR buffer. (3) Weighted-overlap-add variants that make crossfading intrinsic to the block algorithm rather than bolted on: Crochiere's WOLA (50% overlapped analysis/synthesis windows) naturally blends adjacent blocks, and the newest engineering-report contribution I found, TVOLAP (Jaeger, Simmer, Bitzer & Blau, arXiv:2310.00319, 2023), generalizes WOLA to partitioned filters so the whole IR need not be one FFT block: for a 2048-sample IR at 48 kHz, they report OLA at 6.96 MFLOPS/2048-sample latency/clicks, WOLA at 14.06 MFLOPS/2048-sample latency/1024-sample switching latency (smooth), and TVOLAP (512-sample blocks, M=4 partitions) at 14.59 MFLOPS/512-sample latency/256-sample switching latency — i.e. WOLA-quality smoothness at 4x lower audio latency and 4x shorter switching time, and ~13–14x cheaper than naively running two full time-domain convolutions and crossfading (>196 MFLOPS). Müller-Tomfelde's earlier DAFx-01 paper on non-uniform partitioned convolution formalizes the residual hard problem for multi-partition/non-uniform schemes: you can trigger all partitions' updates simultaneously (fastest worst-case response, ~2·N₀, but the output behavior during the multi-module transition is architecturally undefined/messy) or stagger the triggers so every partition finishes its crossfade in the same output block (clean, minimal-duration N₀ crossfade, but the overall response time is capped by the slowest/largest partition's block rate) — there is no free lunch between response latency and transition coherence in non-uniform-partition engines.

On IR morphing/interpolation specifically: naively linearly interpolating (or time-domain crossfading) between two different measured IRs (e.g., two mic positions, or two "sizes") causes comb-filtering, because reflections that arrive at slightly different times in the two IRs destructively cancel when summed — this is why Kearney, Masterson & Boland (AES 35th International Conference, 2009, e-lib ID 15188) split IRs into early reflections + diffuse tail and apply Dynamic Time Warping to temporally align reflections before interpolating them, treating the diffuse tail statistically (critical-band decorrelation) instead. For the specific "size/stretch" case, the cleanest primary-source algorithm I found is Yamaha's US Patent 8,116,470 B2 (Shirakihara, filed 2009/granted 2012): it explicitly rejects the naive approach of multiplying the IR tail by a growing exponential to lengthen reverb time (prior art JP2004-294712), because that also amplifies the noise floor riding on the tail; instead it divides the IR into overlapping Hann-windowed "base blocks," increases the time-gap between block centers by the desired scale factor R, and synthesizes new "interpolation blocks" (by averaging/summing adjacent base blocks, optionally time-reversed or phase-rotated for aural distinctiveness) inserted into the gaps with complementary windowing so overlap-add reconstructs seamlessly — a granular, block-interpolation time-stretch of the IR itself, computed offline before the new IR is loaded into the convolver. This is consistent with how commercial "size" controls actually behave: Altiverb's Room Size/Warp control does genuine IR time-stretching/pitch-shifting via a proprietary IR-specific algorithm (typically constrained to something like a 50–200% ratio), and Waves IR-1 documents Size (acoustic modeling applied only to the early-reflection portion), a T-Time control claimed to stretch/compress reverb time up to 4x while preserving envelope and spectral trend, per-band Damping filters, and a separate "Direct Convolution Length" CPU/latency trade control — all consistent with these being offline IR-recompute-then-reload operations, which is exactly why sweeping "size" during playback in conventional convolution reverbs causes an audible interruption/reload rather than a continuous glide. Zynaptiq's Adaptiverb sidesteps the whole problem by abandoning convolution for a harmonic-tracking oscillator-bank resynthesis engine specifically so the reverb can adapt to input in real time without ever swapping an IR mid-stream. LiquidSonics' Fusion-IR takes a different brute-force route: it runs multiple independently-modulated convolution engines in parallel from multi-sampled captures of a hardware unit's natural chorus/modulation and continuously blends their outputs — essentially generalizing the single old/new-IR output-crossfade to an N-way continuously-blended ensemble, used to simulate a reverb's own inherent modulation rather than to expose a user-automatable size parameter. Finally, a 2025-granted patent by Jonathan S. Abel and colleagues (Callery, Abel, Spratt — US 12,361,921 B2 / US 2023/0018926 A1, priority July 2021) is unusually close to the exact target application: it forms an "evolving sequence of reverberation impulse responses" from segments of the input/other audio ("auto-reverberation"/"cross-reverberation") and explicitly solves the switching-artifact problem either by panning the input signal among several parallel convolvers (each with a different evolving IR, letting old reverb tails decay naturally rather than being cut) or by panning the convolvers' outputs with power-law (constant-power, squared-weights-sum-to-one) crossfades — i.e., it is a modern, patented instance of exactly the output-crossfading-of-parallel-convolvers architecture this project would need, applied specifically to continuously time-varying/input-derived IRs.

## ANGLE: modal

Modal reverberation (Jonathan Abel et al., Stanford CCRMA) is the strongest match to "continuous parameter modulation impossible with raw convolution." A measured IR is fit as a sum of ~300 (spring reverb) to ~1000 (medium room) damped complex sinusoids ("modes"), each implemented as a heterodyne→smooth→re-modulate resonant filter (equivalently a complex one-pole at e^{(jω_m−α_m)/fs}). Cost is ~1 complex MAC + 2 real multiplies per mode per sample plus 6 samples of state per mode — i.e. a few thousand FLOPs/sample independent of decay length, versus direct convolution whose cost scales with IR length (a 2 s IR at 48 kHz ≈ 96k taps). Because each mode is a decoupled low-order recursive filter, its frequency ω_m, damping α_m, and complex gain γ_m are three plain numbers that can be changed every sample with, per the patent, "interactive control with no computational latency" — no crossfading of convolution engines needed. Pitch-shift = uniformly scale all ω_m; time-stretch = resample the mode envelopes on a warped time axis (damping filter applied twice, higher-order envelope filters to suppress tremolo, per the DAFx-15 paper). This is a mature IP family: AES 137th Convention paper (2014), DAFx-15 distortion/pitch paper, a Hammond-tonewheel modal-effects paper (2016), and patents US9,805,704B1 / US11,049,482B1 / US10,262,645B1 / US11,087,733B1, all Jonathan S. Abel. A 2024 arXiv extension (MoD-ART) generalizes modal decomposition to spatially non-uniform decay via acoustic radiance transfer, again claiming real-time-modulatable room parameters.

Sparse-FIR / velvet-noise (Vesa Välimäki et al., Aalto) is the second-best match. Velvet noise (Karjalainen & Järveläinen 2007) is a ternary {-1,0,+1} pulse train at 0.1–1% density; convolving with it is multiplication-free, giving >40 real-time channels on a quad-core SoC (2024 JAES multicore paper). "Late Reverberation Synthesis Using Filtered Velvet Noise" (Appl. Sci. 7(5):483, 2017) cascades a handful of coloration filters with the sparse-FIR skeleton plus Schroeder allpasses, giving continuously adjustable T60(f)/brightness by changing filter coefficients between blocks, claimed >100x cheaper than direct convolution. "Non-Exponential Reverberation Modeling Using Dark Velvet Noise" (JAES 2023, arXiv:2403.20090) time-varies pulse density (2000→500 pulses/s) and probabilistically routes pulses to one of Q dictionary filters, decoupling broadband decay from spectral evolution — both independently, continuously modulatable — and matches a measured hall's T60(f) to 4% mean/8% max error with only 10 filters (vs 14%/28% at 20 filters for the older method), reproducing non-exponential/double-slope decays plain FDNs can't. A 2020 DAFx paper hybridizes velvet noise into an FDN specifically to fix FDN's slow initial echo build-up while keeping smooth per-band modulatable decay.

FDN-fitting via differentiable programming turns a target IR into a compact, smoothly-modulatable analytic FDN through offline gradient descent (not real-time). Dal Santo/Prawda/Schlecht/Välimäki (DAFx23, journal extension arXiv:2402.11216, EURASIP JASMP 2025) fit feedback matrix + I/O gains with as few as 4-6 delay lines by minimizing spectral-flatness + echo-density loss. Mezza/Giampiccolo/De Sena/Bernardini (EURASIP JASMP 2024, arXiv:2404.00082) additionally make delay-line lengths trainable (Pei-Lai fractional delay filters) via a differentiable "Soft Echo Density Profile" loss, fitting 3 MIT-survey RIRs (T60 0.2–1.2 s) to T60 errors of roughly 2-7%, N=6 delay lines, ~800-1000 gradient iterations to converge (5.7-42 s/iteration on a V100). (Note: the paper's own comparative FLOPS figures for FDN-vs-convolution as extracted appeared internally inconsistent — verify directly against the PDF before quoting a specific multiplier.) Newer 2025 work (Ibnyahya & Reiss, arXiv:2511.20380) shares SOS/parametric-EQ parameters across delay lines to cut optimization cost, and Gerami & Duraiswami (arXiv:2510.00238) target real-time listener-movement BRIR rendering. Once fit, the resulting handful of parameters (delay lengths, feedback-matrix angle, per-band gain/T60) are exactly the kind of low-dimensional, physically meaningful knobs that a plugin can crossfade/automate live.

The common theoretical root is Goodwin & Vetterli's matching pursuit with damped sinusoids (ICASSP 1997, Munich, vol.3 pp.2037-2040; journal version IEEE Trans. Signal Processing 47(7):1890-1902, 1999), which greedily decomposes any signal (including a measured IR) into complex damped-sinusoid atoms via recursive filter banks + FFT — the general-purpose ancestor of Abel's room-specific modal fit. Across all these representations, the modulation story is the same: because each atom/mode/delay-line parameter is a decoupled low-order recursive element, changing (frequency, damping, gain) at control- or sample-rate produces no clicks, unlike raw (even partitioned) convolution which needs whole-engine crossfades to change the IR. Välimäki, Parker, Savioja, Smith & Abel's "Fifty Years of Artificial Reverberation" (IEEE TASLP 20(5):1421-1448, 2012) is the best single survey tying delay-network, convolution, and physical/modal families together for an implementer choosing among them.

## ANGLE: nonlinear

Nonlinear convolution beyond simple IR-switching splits into two largely separate research lineages that a next-gen engine needs to fuse: (A) measurement/identification of input-level-dependent kernels (Farina's ESS/diagonal-Volterra line; Novak's synchronized-swept-sine/generalized-Hammerstein line), and (B) click-free real-time modulation of a running convolution's filter (Brandtsegg/Saue; Wefers/Vorländer; Jaeger et al.). Almost nothing published does both natively, per-sample, in real time — the closest is Farina's 2007 "not-linear, not-time-invariant convolver" and a 2023 adaptive-LMS diagonal-Volterra estimator that updates kernels during normal operation.

Farina, Bellini & Armelloni (AES 110th Convention, Paper 5359, Amsterdam, May 2001, "Non-Linear Convolution: A New Approach for the Auralization of Distorting Systems") is the foundational paper, building on Farina's ESS measurement method (AES 108th Convention, Paper 5093, Paris, Feb 2000, "Simultaneous Measurement of Impulse Response and Distortion with a Swept-Sine Technique"). The DUT is excited with an exponential sine sweep x(t)=sin[ω1·L·(e^(t/L)−1)], L=T/ln(f2/f1); deconvolving with a time-reversed, +6 dB/oct pre-equalized inverse filter yields ONE impulse response containing time-separated harmonic-order responses h'_n, each preceding the linear IR by Δt=L·ln(n) samples. Assuming a Hammerstein structure (memoryless nonlinearity → LTI block with memory), the full multidimensional Volterra kernels collapse to "diagonal" 1-D kernels h_n solved from a closed-form linear system built from sin^n(ω) trig identities — the paper gives the exact 5th-order solution (H1=H'1+3H'3+5H'5; H2=2jH'2+8jH'4; H3=−4H'3−20H'5; H4=−8jH'4; H5=16H'5). Playback reconstructs y(n)=Σ hk(i)·x^k(n−i): each order's kernel convolves with the input signal raised to that power — genuinely input-amplitude-dependent, not IR-switching — implemented as a bank of parallel FFT/select-save (Soo–Pang partitioned) convolutions with the input pre-raised to the k-th power per branch. Measurement is offline; playback is real time on ordinary hardware even for IRs of "ten thousands of coefficients." Real-time commercial embodiment: Acustica Audio's Nebula plugin (cited in Farina & A. Farina, AES 123rd Convention, Paper 7295, New York, Oct 2007, "Real-Time Auralization Employing a Not-Linear, Not-Time-Invariant Convolver"), supporting up to 5th-order kernels via overlap-save partitioned convolution; the same paper is the key precedent for COMBINING nonlinearity with time-variance — it re-runs the ESS measurement repeatedly (across a time cycle or at different drive amplitudes) and cross-fades/updates the diagonal-kernel sets, explicitly targeting "not-time-invariant" behavior. A closely related 2005 paper, Farina & Armelloni, "Emulation of Not-Linear, Time-Variant Devices by the Convolution Technique" (AES Italian Section Annual Meeting, Paper 05014, Nov 2005), is the direct precursor. University of Bologna researchers patented a refined version: US 9,171,534 B2, "Method for Artificially Reproducing an Output Signal of a Non-Linear Time Invariant System," inventors Lamberto Tronchin & Andrea Venturi, assignee Alma Mater Studiorum–Università di Bologna, PCT filed 2010-12-23 (priority IT MO2009A000313, 2009-12-23), granted 2015-10-27 — it patents the ESS→diagonal-Volterra pipeline plus a Nelson–Kirkeby-method phase-correction FIR filter per harmonic branch (needed because the raw deconvolution has a flat magnitude but wrong phase relative to a true impulse) — 6 claims, cites Farina 2005 and Novak 2009 (DAFx-09) as prior art.

The Novak/Simon/Lotton/Gilbert line (Le Mans, France) reworked Farina's ESS into an identifiable model rather than just a measurement tool. Key papers: Novak, Simon, Kadlec & Lotton, "Nonlinear System Identification Using Exponential Swept-Sine Signal" (IEEE Trans. Instrum. Meas., first published online 2009-10-29); Novak, Simon, Kadlec & Lotton, DAFx-09 (Como, 2009), "Modeling of Nonlinear Audio Systems using Swept-Sine Signals: Application to Audio Effects"; Novak, Simon, Lotton & Gilbert, DAFx-10 (Graz, Sept 2010), "Chebyshev Model and Synchronized Swept Sine Method in Nonlinear Audio Effect Modeling" (read in full: uses a synchronized/rounded sweep length L=Round(T·f1/ln(f2/f1))/f1 so harmonic separations land on exact sample boundaries; validated on a real overdrive pedal at fs=192kHz, sweep 1Hz–10kHz, order N=9, giving MSE=4e−5 V on a 500Hz sine and MSE=2e−4 V on real guitar audio); and the mature summary Novak, Lotton & Simon, J. Audio Eng. Soc. 63(10):786–798, 2015, "Synchronized Swept-Sine: Theory, Application, and Implementation" (DOI 10.17743/jaes.2015.0071) plus the EURASIP J. Adv. Signal Process. 2010:793816 paper "Analysis, Synthesis, and Classification of Nonlinear Systems Using Synchronized Swept-Sine Method for Audio Effects." The output is a Generalized (polynomial or Chebyshev) Hammerstein model: N parallel branches, each a static nonlinearity (power-series term or Chebyshev polynomial Tn, chosen so pure n-th harmonics are cleanly generated) followed by a linear filter Hn(f) derived from the higher-harmonic frequency responses via a transformation matrix A(n,m). This is again an OFFLINE identification procedure (single sweep, FFT-based deconvolution, MATLAB/Python reference code at ant-novak.com/pages/sss/), but the resulting parallel-Hammerstein structure is cheap enough to run in real time for playback/emulation (this is essentially the architecture behind most commercial "amp-in-a-box"/pedal modeling plugins, though none of the vendor products were confirmed as citing Novak directly).

A directly relevant 2023 advance closes part of the gap toward true real-time, self-updating input-dependent kernels: Pinardi et al., "Estimation of Diagonal Volterra Kernels of an Audio System During Normal Operation with Multiple Least Mean Squares Adaptive Filters" (I3DA — Immersive and 3D Audio conference, 2023). Instead of a dedicated ESS calibration signal, it runs parallel LMS adaptive filters to estimate the diagonal Volterra kernels continuously from ordinary program material — online adaptive nonlinear-kernel tracking, the natural next step toward IRs that depend on and adapt to live input rather than a one-time offline sweep. Also worth flagging as an efficient non-measurement alternative: Hélie, T., DAFx-06, Montreal, "On the Use of Volterra Series for Real-Time Simulations of Weakly Nonlinear Analog Audio Devices: Application to the Moog Ladder Filter" — derives Volterra kernels analytically from a circuit's governing ODEs rather than from swept-sine measurement, giving an exact, real-time-efficient nonlinear filter structure when a circuit model is available.

For limitation (1) — modulating IR-altering parameters without clicks — the state of the art is independent of the nonlinear-Volterra work and directly transferable to any input-dependent-IR scheme needing to blend or swap kernel sets in real time. Brandtsegg & Saue, DAFx-17 (Edinburgh, Sept 2017), "Live Convolution with Time-Variant Impulse Response" (read in full) introduces stepwise/incremental replacement of IR partitions in a running uniformly-partitioned frequency-domain convolver (Csound `liveconv` opcode, C implementation, open source): loading and unloading proceeds partition-by-partition exploiting the property that partition (i,j) only contributes to output interval Y_{i+j} — so a full IR swap is spread over exactly N−1 samples (or P−1 partitions) at ZERO extra computational cost versus static convolution (no crossfade multiply/add needed), measured SNR of 67.2 dB between stepwise-replacement output and a reference of two overlapped time-invariant convolutions. Their follow-up, Brandtsegg & Saue, Applied Sciences (MDPI) 8(1):103, 2018, "Live Convolution with Time-Varying Filters," extends this with a second artifact-free update method. The theoretical crossfading alternative, cited by both later papers as the standard baseline, is Wefers & Vorländer, Forum Acousticum 2014, "Efficient Time-Varying FIR Filtering Using Crossfading Implemented in the DFT Domain" (crossfades entirely in the frequency domain, saving one inverse FFT versus naive dual-convolution crossfade); Wefers' 2015 RWTH Aachen PhD dissertation ("Partitioned Convolution Algorithms for Real-Time Auralization," Logos Verlag Berlin) is the standard survey of OLA/OLS/POLA/POLS variants. The current best-documented algorithm with hard numbers is Jaeger, Simmer, Bitzer & Blau, arXiv:2310.00319 (30 Sep 2023), "Time-Variant Overlap-Add in Partitions" (TVOLAP; read in full, open-source Python/C++/MATLAB at github.com/TGM-Oldenburg/TVOLAP, LGPL): partitions both IR and input into 2L-sample Hann-windowed blocks, so switching all M partition spectra swaps the whole time-varying filter atomically with switching latency = N_IR/(2·M·fs) — for a 2048-tap IR with block 2L=512 (M=4 partitions) at 48kHz this is a 256-sample switching latency and 512-sample audio latency at 14.59 MFLOPS (vs WOLA's 1024-sample switching latency at 14.06 MFLOPS for the same audio latency, or naive dual-time-domain-convolution-with-crossfade at >196 MFLOPS with zero audio latency but unbounded switching cost); OLA/OLS instantaneous-switch produce audible clicks, WOLA/TVOLAP smoothly crossfade. This paper is the most quotable modern reference for "how to change convolution-reverb IR parameters continuously without clicks, with exact FLOPS/latency tradeoffs" — directly answering limitation (1), independent of nonlinearity.

For neural/differentiable context (per instructions, background only): Differentiable Artificial Reverberation surveys/methods (Lee et al., IEEE/ACM Trans. Audio Speech Lang. Process., 2022, arXiv:2105.13940; De Santo, Prawda, Schlecht & Välimäki, DAFx-23, "Differentiable Feedback Delay Network for Colorless Reverberation") make FDN-style artificial reverb parameters gradient-optimizable, mostly for offline training/fitting rather than literal per-sample dynamic convolution, though FDN parameter updates are cheap enough to be real-time-capable once trained. No source found applies ML "dynamic convolution" (input-conditioned kernel generation, à la Chen et al. CVPR 2020) to convolution-reverb audio effects specifically — the closest analogues are neural IR-generation-on-demand systems (e.g., US Patent 12,198,715, "System and method for generating impulse responses using neural networks," for real-time listener-position-dependent BRIR synthesis) which regenerate a whole IR per new condition rather than conditioning on instantaneous input amplitude; this is a genuine white-space the target plugin could occupy as a first.
