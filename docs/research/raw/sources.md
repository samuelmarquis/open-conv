This is Michael Kemp's foundational Dynamic Convolution paper describing measurement of non-linear audio devices (valve EQ, tape machine, guitar amp/speaker, compressors) via step-impulse trains at 128 linearly-spaced amplitude levels, and real-time simulation via input-amplitude-dependent selection/interpolation among the resulting impulse response set — directly the "input-level-dependent IR crossfade" prior art the plugin design needs to engage with (and differentiate from, since it is per-sample level-selection among pre-measured static IRs rather than smooth real-time morphing of a single continuously-parameterized IR).

The core algorithm: standard LTI convolution y(n)=Σx(n-k)h(k) (eq.1) is generalized by measuring M (e.g. 128) impulse responses h_1..h_M at linearly spaced impulse amplitudes, each normalized to peak value fs/(fs−m·d) where d=fs/128. A selector S(x(n))=1+⌊|x(n)|/(fs/M)⌋ picks a response per input-sample magnitude (eq.2), and linear interpolation via fraction p(x(n))=(|x(n)| mod (fs/M))/(fs/M) blends the two nearest responses (eq.3): y(n)=Σx(n-k)·[p(x(n))·h_{S(x(n-k))}(k) + (1−p(x(n)))·h_{S(x(n-k))−1}(k)]. A dummy h_0=h_1 avoids special-casing low-level samples. Measurement uses a step (not unit) impulse for more low-frequency energy, converted to unit IR by first-order differencing y_n=x_n−x_{n-1}; a 4000-sample capture window per step, 8000-sample settling, all multiples of 1000, double-precision float arithmetic throughout, final quarter-length linear-ramp window to kill residual tail.

Noise handling: since the lowest-level IR sits ~40dB below peak, three complementary techniques are given — (a) noise-floor splicing: measure noise floor from a quiet section, walk a high-level IR down to where it nears the noise floor, then splice in matching data from the next-higher-level IR with cross-fades at the splice boundaries, repeating down to the lowest level; (b) coherent averaging of repeated measurements at a given level (3dB noise reduction per doubling of averaged samples, works for both random and non-synchronous repetitive noise); (c) post-hoc discarding of low-level IRs below ~12dB from peak for devices that are "essentially linear" there, replicating the lowest retained IR for all quieter inputs.

Extensions: the selector is generalized from S(x(n)) (single sample) to S(x,n) (dependent on signal history/neighborhood) — the paper explicitly states they "have done some tests using an envelope of the input signal as the selection criterion." A sign-dependent extension is also proposed: measuring separate IR sets from positive- and negative-going step impulses and selecting by sign as well as magnitude. Section 5 gives the multi-dimensional extension: take multiple IR sets at different device settings (a second control dimension — user parameter, LFO/oscillator, or compressor gain-reduction amount) and use bilinear interpolation across amplitude (dim 1) and the second control dimension.

Hardware: the Sintefex FX8000 "Audio Effects Replicator" used 60MHz Analog Devices ADSP-21065L SHARC chips. Design target: 2048 taps/channel real-time at up to 50kHz (with 100kHz supported at reduced IR length), 24-bit internal precision, ≥20-bit converters, +30dBu test signal capability. Per-tap inner loop: fetch partial sum, fetch IR element A, fetch IR element B, MAC with A, MAC with B, write partial sum — 2 memory accesses + 1 MAC per clock lets the SHARC do this in 2 clocks/tap-pair; 60MHz → 512 steps in ~17µs, giving 50kHz with a 15% safety margin for loop setup. Two rows of 4 SHARCs cascaded (partial sums passed processor-to-processor per input sample) yield stereo at 2048 taps/channel; a further coordinating SHARC demuxes stereo, precomputes selector/interpolation pointers, and runs EQ/compression. Up to 4 stereo sections per chassis for multichannel/surround. Earlier software prototypes: "FIRST" offline tool on a 90MHz Pentium (L up to 1000 taps, hours to process 30s of audio) and "SFXDEM" real-time demo on 300MHz Pentium-II achieving only L≈150 mono / ~75 stereo in real time at 44.1kHz — motivating the dedicated SHARC hardware. Key efficiency claim: the 2-multiply/2-accumulate-per-tap dynamic convolution runs at "half the speed of a simple multiply accumulate convolution operation... which since it needs two multiplies and two accumulates per step means we can achieve close to 100% efficiency from a DSP chip" — i.e. despite doing 2x the MACs of plain convolution, the SHARC's dual-MAC/dual-fetch architecture absorbs it with no wasted cycles.

Compressor-specific measurement protocol (Section 7): to characterize devices whose response depends on very recent history (compressors/limiters) rather than treating this as violating the LTI-per-sample assumption, they set slow attack/decay, play a fixed-level conditioning signal between impulses until compressor gain reaches a target value, then fire the impulse — yielding a family of IRs indexed by attenuation/gain-reduction amount, later selected via the bilinear multi-dimensional scheme with gain reduction as the second control axis.

- Eq.2 dynamic convolution: y(n) = Σ_{k=0}^{L-1} x(n-k)·h_{S(x(n-k))}(k), where S is evaluated per-tap using the historical sample x(n-k), not just x(n)
- Selector: S(x(n)) = 1 + floor(|x(n)| / (fs/M)); clamp |x(n)| to fs−1 when x(n)=−fs to match positive-side max; assumes fs exactly divisible by M
- Interpolation fraction: p(x(n)) = (|x(n)| mod (fs/M)) / (fs/M)
- Eq.3 full simulation: y(n) = Σ_{k=0}^{L-1} x(n-k)·[p(x(n))·h_{S(x(n-k))}(k) + (1−p(x(n)))·h_{S(x(n-k))−1}(k)]; define h_0 := h_1 so the S−1 index never needs special-casing at low levels
- IR normalization: the m-th response, measured at step amplitude (fs − m·d), is scaled by factor fs/(fs − m·d) to bring all IRs to common peak-referenced level before use
- Measurement stimulus: alternating polarity step train — negative to −fs/2 held 8000 samples, positive transition of fs (first excitation), hold 4000 samples for IR capture, negative to −(fs−d)/2, hold 4000 samples to stabilize, positive transition of (fs−d), repeat with decreasing step sizes (fs−2d), (fs−3d)... down toward zero
- Unit-impulse derivation from step response via first-order differencing: y_n = x_n − x_{n-1}; requires full double-precision floating point arithmetic to preserve dynamic range/precision through this and subsequent processing
- Real-time engine optimization: rather than looping per output sample and reselecting IR pairs for each history sample, the implementation loops once per input sample x(n), determines one selector/interpolation pair up front, splits x(n) into two scaled values s1,s2 (s1+s2=y(n) contribution proportion) via p(x(n)), then does a single pass of the length-L impulse buffer performing two MACs per tap (against h_S and h_{S-1}) accumulating into a sliding partial-sum output buffer (Figure 7's partial-sum accumulator structure, akin to overlap of a direct-form transversal filter with a moving buffer, zero-filled at the tail, circular reuse after each y(n) is drained)
- Multi-dimensional/bilinear extension: for a second control axis c (user knob, LFO, or compressor gain reduction), measure whole IR-level ladders h_{m,c} at multiple settings of c; at runtime bilinearly interpolate across (amplitude-selector-index, c-index) using two interpolation fractions analogous to p(x(n))

- Sign-dependent extension (unimplemented in paper, proposed): measure IR sets from both positive-going and negative-going step impulses; select using sign(x(n-k)) in addition to |x(n-k)| magnitude bin

This is the foundational dynamic/nonlinear convolution patent underlying Sintefex's amplitude-dependent reverb technology (later licensed by Focusrite). Kemp's method stores a bank of impulse responses (IRs) captured at a graduated series of test-signal amplitudes — typically 128 or 256 levels spanning full scale down to roughly 1/128–1/256 of maximum (for a 16-bit system: 32768, 32512, 32256, ... down to 256) — rather than a single LTI impulse response. At runtime the instantaneous input sample magnitude (or, in a generalized variant, an attack/decay envelope tracker rather than raw instantaneous magnitude) is used to select which two bracketing IRs to apply and in what proportion, effectively implementing input-amplitude-dependent convolution: a crude piecewise-linear approximation to a nonlinear, level-dependent system built from linear building blocks.

The core selection mechanism is a two-threshold bracket-and-blend scheme: the sample magnitude is compared against adjacent quantized thresholds Tn ≤ |S| < Tn+1, a fractional position k within the bracket is computed, and the sample is split into two scaled copies F1 = S×(1−k) and F2 = S×k that are each convolved with their respective bracket's IR and summed — giving proportional/continuous blending between adjacent-amplitude IRs rather than a hard switch, which is the key anti-click mechanism for parameter-space crossfading. The patent explicitly frames this as applicable to a "predetermined range" of amplitudes with proportions "dependent on the position of the amplitude ... within the predetermined range," i.e., a general amplitude-bracket crossfade architecture, not restricted to any single interpolation curve.

Two extensions matter for the design: (1) a noise-floor splicing/repair technique (FIG. 19) where the low-amplitude (hence low-SNR) IRs are cleaned by computing an RMS envelope of a captured (or synthetic) response, comparing it to a noise-floor threshold, and softly crossfading in higher-amplitude-derived IR content below that threshold (recommended ~50% above noise floor, applied progressively down to about −12 dB below max) — solving the practical problem that quiet-level IRs are inherently noisy/unusable without this splice; and (2) an envelope-based (attack/decay) generalization of the selector (FIG. 21) that replaces raw per-sample |S| with a leaky-integrator/peak-follower envelope (attack ≈10, decay ≈1000 as exponential asymptotic constants) before bracket lookup, which smooths selection against sample-to-sample noise and is the mechanism most directly relevant to "input-level-dependent IR selection without artifacts," at the cost of optionally delaying the input by a buffer of n prior samples to time-align output with the smoothed envelope decision (added latency ∝ attack/decay time constant, not sample-exact).

Computationally, the patent quotes concrete cost figures: for a 5,000-tap IR, each output sample requires accumulating "5,000 values of input sample multiplied with 5,000 impulse response samples, or 250 million operations per second" at a 50 kHz sample rate — i.e., essentially double the naive single-IR convolution's MAC count in the crossfade region only if two IRs are summed for the same output sample (the F1/F2 split means two convolution accumulations run per bracketed sample rather than one). The reference architecture in FIG. 13 partitions this across DSPs — one input-side DSP computing the per-sample bracket index and F1/F2 split into circular buffers, and N further DSPs each handling M/N of the multiply-accumulate steps (worked example: 10 DSPs × 500 steps for a 5,000-tap IR) — establishing that this is a straightforward SIMD/partition-parallel direct-convolution architecture, not FFT/partitioned-convolution based, which matters for latency (no FFT block latency, but full 5,000-tap direct-form MAC cost per sample) versus a modern partitioned/uniform or non-uniform frequency-domain convolution engine.

ALGORITHM DETAILS:
- Two-threshold bracket lookup: given adjacent quantized thresholds Tn ≤ |S| < Tn+1, compute k = (|S|-Tn)/(Tn+1-Tn); split sample into F1 = S×(1−k) applied to lower-bracket IR and F2 = S×k applied to upper-bracket IR; F1+F2 = S (energy/amplitude-conserving split).
- Per-sample pipeline (FIG. 9-11): compute bracket index A(J) and F1/F2 once per input sample, push into circular buffers; convolution engine (FIG. 12) then for each output sample loops J=0..M-1 accumulating S_OUT += F1[J]×IR1[A(J)+J] + F2[J]×IR2[A(J)+J+M] — i.e. two parallel direct-form convolutions (one per bracketing IR) summed per output sample, giving continuous crossfade as input amplitude wanders between IR levels/bins over time.
- IR capture protocol: apply a step (or later, an impulse, FIG. 18) test signal at each of the graduated amplitudes to the physical/target device being modeled, capture its response, then normalize each captured IR by (max test amplitude / actual test amplitude) so all IRs are comparable in the bank; a preliminary distortion check derives a global attenuation factor applied uniformly to the whole IR set if clipping/distortion is detected at high test levels.
- Noise-floor splice (FIG. 19): compute envelope 91 as RMS-weighted local average (window ~400-500 samples) of a given low-amplitude IR; compare against a noise-floor threshold 92; wherever envelope is below threshold, replace/blend that region using IR_out = IR_low×(1−w) + IR_high×w with a soft/progressive crossfade weight w derived from how far below threshold, extended from a higher (cleaner) sample level down to the lowest usable level (~-12dB below max) — this repairs the low-level IRs that are otherwise too noisy to use directly, before they are placed in the bank.
- Envelope-driven selector generalization (FIG. 21): env ← env×decay_factor each sample (leaky decay); v ← |sample|; if v > env then env ← env + (v−env)×attack_factor (fast attack toward louder peaks); use env instead of |S| for the bracket/threshold lookup — turns the selector into an asymmetric attack/decay follower akin to a compressor's level detector, smoothing bracket transitions against instantaneous sample noise; optional delay line of n prior samples lets the dry/convolution path be re-aligned to the smoothed decision, trading latency for selection stability.
- Multi-DSP parallelization (FIG. 13): DSP1 performs the per-sample preprocessing (bracket/threshold determination, F1/F2 computation) and writes into shared circular buffers; DSPs 2..N each own a contiguous M/N-length slice of the convolution accumulation (e.g., 10 DSPs x 500 taps for M=5000) computing partial sums independently; DSP1 (or a dedicated summer) adds the N partial sums to form the final output sample — a straightforward tap-partitioned SIMD/multi-core direct convolution, with no FFT or partitioned block processing described, implying full per-sample MAC cost and no FFT-induced block latency, but higher raw compute versus a partitioned frequency-domain convolver of equivalent tap count.

WO2000028521A1 ("Audio dynamic control effects synthesiser with or without analyser," Michael Joseph Kemp, assignee Sintefex Audio Lda; priority 11 Nov 1998, filed 11 Nov 1999, published 18 May 2000) is the dynamics-processor-specific companion to Kemp's convolution/impulse-response patents (referencing prior PCT GB97/02159, WO98/07114). It documents a full measurement-and-synthesis pipeline for capturing the behavior of real compressors/limiters (gain characteristic, attack/release time constants, and non-linear distortion via impulse responses) and reproducing them at runtime via a two-axis interpolation scheme — one axis over signal amplitude/gain-reduction amount, one axis over the compression-ratio/gain-characteristic family — so the "IR selection changes smoothly with input level" problem the plugin project cares about is solved here at the level of gain curves and companded FIR sets rather than pure IR morphing.

The measurement procedure is concrete and reproducible: gain characteristics are captured by stepping a 1 kHz sine input from -40 dBm to 0 dBm in 1 dB steps (optionally coarser, since intermediate points are recoverable by interpolation) for several ratio settings (patent gives illustrative example ratios like 1.5:1, 5:1, 20:1 at -20 dB threshold), waiting for output stability to within 0.1 dB over a 100 ms window before logging. Time constants are extracted with the device forced to 20:1 ratio / -20 dBm threshold / fastest attack-release, stepping the input 0 dBm -> -20 dBm and back while sampling the output every cycle until it settles to within 0.1 dB/second, giving empirical attack and release envelopes rather than assumed one-pole time constants. Non-linearity/distortion capture uses an impulse-response method: with the device set to slow attack/decay and a fixed high ratio (20:1), a sine tone is held until gain stabilizes, removed over interval T1, allowed to settle for T2 (~100 samples at 48 kHz), then a step impulse is applied and the response recorded for a window T3 (~200 samples) — short enough that the compressor's own gain has not measurably moved during capture, yielding a near-instantaneous-condition IR at that specific gain-reduction operating point. This whole capture is repeated at multiple impulse amplitudes and multiple gain-reduction points to build a family of FIRs indexed by level/gain.

At runtime the system computes an instantaneous envelope via a simple two-branch peak/RMS-style detector (attack coefficient k1, release coefficient k2, each derived from user or measured time constants via (1-k1)^r = exp(-1/T1), k2^r = exp(-1/T2), r = sample rate), looks up/interpolates the gain characteristic to find the ratio-consistent target gain, then reconstructs the actual FIR to convolve by 2D bilinear interpolation: coefficient j blends between two neighboring measured FIR sets (with intrinsic gains a dB and b dB) so that the resulting linear gain Mg = j*Ma + (1-j)*Mb matches the desired gain G exactly (solved as j = (Mg-Mb)/(Ma-Mb)), while coefficient k blends within/between adjacent impulse-response sample sets for the amplitude axis, and the same j/k structure is used per-convolution-step (4 interpolation data points per step cited in the figure-9 description) so the filter can move continuously in level-space without discrete switches. Two gain-embedding strategies are given: leave the compressor's own attenuation baked into each measured FIR (so j alone reproduces the curve, with extra multiplicative correction F = G - A dB only needed outside the measured range), or normalize all captured FIRs to unity gain and apply the gain reduction as a separate multiplicative stage (element 113) driven independently by the interpolated gain-characteristic value — decoupling "what the nonlinearity/coloration sounds like at this gain-reduction point" from "how much gain reduction to apply," which is directly reusable for a level-dependent-IR reverb: cross-fade/interpolate between amplitude-indexed IR families using the same bilinear j,k scheme, using a normalized-FIR + separate gain-multiplier architecture to avoid conflating spectral/nonlinear character changes with overall loudness changes as input level shifts. Ratio interpolation itself (independent of the FIR-selection axis) is handled by rational rather than linear blending: coefficient a = N(M-R)/(R(M-N)) for interpolating between measured ratios N:1 and M:1, degenerating to a = N/R when extrapolating toward infinite ratio (true limiting) and a = (M-R)/(R(M-1)) when extrapolating toward 1:1 (unity/no compression) — i.e. the patent explicitly works in gain-ratio (1/ratio) space rather than dB-linear space for this axis because compression ratio behaves reciprocally, a subtlety worth replicating if the reverb design ever parameterizes "amount of level-dependence" as a ratio-like knob.

A second interpolation factor k blends between adjacent impulse-response sample points along the amplitude axis (within-IR interpolation), giving a genuine two-axis (j,k) bilinear grid over (gain-reduction level x impulse-response family).
- Ratio-curve interpolation (independent of the FIR/IR axis): for desired ratio R between measured ratios N:1 and M:1, with X,Y the output levels from the M:1 and N:1 curves respectively at a given input level: a = N(M-R)/(R(M-N)) [Eq.1], Z = (1-a)X + aY [Eq.2] (i.e., linear cross-fade of the two output-level curves using a ratio-derived, non-linear-in-R coefficient). This converts an M-way (64-way) level-switched/interpolated convolution bank into a fixed sum of K (with K reported as small, ~1–3 components sufficing for a guitar/bass preamp case) waveshaper-gated FIR convolutions — cutting MAC count and stored-IR memory roughly in proportion to K/M versus the naive Kemp dynamic-convolution implementation.

For long RIRs under non-uniform partitioning (NUPOLS/Gardner-style multi-block-size schemes), the thesis reproduces and extends Müller-Tomfelde's DAFx-01 (2001) two exchange strategies: "coherent exchange" (all segments' triggers aligned so the whole filter transitions in one crossfade cycle — best case fast, but worst-case response latency can be as long as the coarsest segment's period, e.g. the whole multi-cycle Gardner schedule) vs "asynchronous exchange" (each segment swaps immediately at its own next execution, giving constant low response time ~1 cycle but an uneven/"starved" transition unless larger crossfades are used on the longer segments). Wefers notes partition segment-length choice (power-of-two, small LCM) directly trades off exchange-latency predictability against coherence — a load-bearing design constraint for a size/stretch-morphing plugin using non-uniform partitioning.

- Frequency-domain crossfade (variant 3, Wefers's own method): requires K to be an integer multiple of 2B (2B | K, so P=K/(2B) is an integer number of periods) and the crossfade must span a FULL block, L=B. Cyclically shift the zero-padded filter before transformation so valid OLS output samples sit at the start of the buffer, freeing the K-B 'discard zone' samples to host a periodic extension of the fade envelope. Extended envelopes: f~out(n)=sin^2(pi n P/K)=1/2 - 1/2 cos(2pi n P/K); f~in(n)=cos^2(pi n P/K)=1/2+1/2 cos(2pi n P/K) (Eqs.4.41-4.42), whose K-point DFTs have only 3 nonzero real coefficients each (at bins -P,0,+P; Eqs.4.46-4.47), turning the crossfade into a sparse 3-tap circular convolution in the DFT domain (Eq.4.50): Y(k) = (K/2)[Y0(k)+Y1(k)] + (1/2)[(Y1(k+P)-Y0(k+P)) + (Y1(k-P)-Y0(k-P))]. Cost: T_FD-CROSSFADE(K) = 3*T_ADD(2K)+2*T_SUB(2K)+T_MUL(2K) = 12K real ops total (Eq.4.51); total stream cost Tstream_TV3 = (1/B)[T_FFT(K)+2*T_CMUL(K)+T_FD-CROSSFADE(K)+T_IFFT(K)] (Eq.4.52) — only ONE inverse FFT needed (vs two for variant 1/2).
- Filter/sequential-cascade merging: cascaded filters h(n)=h0*h1*...*h(M-1) can be pre-multiplied in the frequency domain (H(k)=prod H(i)(k)) requiring transform size K >= B + (sum Ni - M) for alias-free linear convolution (Eq.4.23); parallel filter sums similarly merge to H(k)=sum H(i)(k) with K>=B+max(Ni)-1 (Eq.4.29) — relevant to combining a size/pitch/damping-modified IR chain into one spectrum before convolution.
- Non-uniform partition exchange scheduling (Müller-Tomfelde 2001, reproduced/extended in Ch.6.11): 'coherent exchange' triggers all segments to switch simultaneously at the next fully-parallel execution point (small least-common-multiple of segment lengths minimizes worst-case wait); 'asynchronous exchange' triggers each segment's swap at its own immediate next execution, giving constant ~1-block-cycle response time but a transition that can 'starve' (visually gray/incomplete) unless crossfade length is scaled per-segment size. Recommends power-of-two sub-filter lengths Li (small LCM) to keep coherent-exchange worst-case response time bounded.

Guillermo García (Creative ATC / CCRMA Stanford), "Optimal Filter Partition for Efficient Convolution with Short Input/Output Delay," AES 113th Convention, Los Angeles, Oct 5-8 2002, Convention Paper 5660. The paper formalizes the non-uniform FDL (frequency-domain delay line) partitioning problem as a shortest-path/dynamic-programming search solved with the Viterbi algorithm, and is the direct ancestor of the partitioning schemes used in Wefers' PhD thesis and in most modern zero/low-latency convolution engines (JUCE dsp::Convolution, jconvolver descendants, etc.).

Core contribution: rather than the double-FDL heuristic (Gardner 1995) or fixed non-uniform block-doubling series (also Gardner), García casts the search over ALL possible partitions of a T-sample IR into a sequence of FDLs (each FDL = a contiguous run of same-size blocks, block sizes constrained to powers of two and non-decreasing along the IR) as a state-sequence optimization. States are defined per pointer position t (at multiples of the header block size N) as a pair [S.Q] = "currently inside a block of size S*N, at the Q-th N-sample sub-fraction of it." Legal transitions are: (a) same-block sub-fraction advance ([X.Q]->[X.Q+1], cost 0), (b) closing a block and starting a new same-size block ([X.X]->[X.1], cost = one spectral-multiply-add = 4 madds), (c) closing a block and jumping to a strictly larger block size Y>X, starting a new FDL ([X.X]->[Y.1], cost = one direct FFT + one spectral multiply-add + one inverse FFT = 4k·log2(2YN) + 4, reducible to (1+2/P)k·log2(2YN)+4 for P parallel channels when Y=2X via FFT split-radix half-spectrum reuse). Two structural constraints prune the state space to make Viterbi tractable: block sizes must be powers of two, and a block cannot start before its own length into the IR (i.e., no block of size S can occur earlier than sample S), and block size sequence along the IR must be non-decreasing.

The Viterbi recursion is standard: δ_t(i) = min_j[δ_{t-1}(j) + cost(j→i)], Φ_t(i) = argmin_j[...], initialized at δ_0(0) = 4k·log2(2N)+4 for the forced starting state [1.1] (partition must begin with a header block of size N = the latency spec), and the optimal path recovered by backtracking Φ from argmin_i δ_{T/N-1}(i). This is an efficient, comprehensive, exact-optimum, dynamic-programming replacement for the double-FDL heuristic, run once (or periodically) whenever the IR length changes — directly relevant to the plugin's need to reschedule FDL partitions on the fly when IR length/stretch is modulated.

Concrete numeric example (T=131072 samples ≈ 2.97s @44.1kHz, latency spec N=256 samples/5.8ms, k=1.5): optimal 3-FDL partition found by the algorithm = 8 blocks of 256 samples + 7 blocks of 2048 samples + 7 blocks of 16384 samples, cost 304 madds/output-sample. Comparisons at the same T,N,k: single monolithic 131072-pt FFT block (impractical, 3s latency) = 112 madds; uniform-partition MBUP = 16411 madds; single-FDL = 2102 madds; Gardner's non-uniform block-doubling minimum-cost (MBMC) = 769 madds; Gardner's uniform-processor-load version (MBUL) = 964 madds. So the Viterbi-optimal multi-FDL partition is >2x more efficient than Gardner's non-uniform partition and ~7x more efficient than single-FDL. Search-space size at this T,N: 70,055,503 possible partitions — cost of exhaustive search ranges 308 (near-optimal) to 2402 madds (worst partition); a naively "trivial" single-256-sample-block-FDL partition costs 2122 madds, underscoring that ad hoc partitions are far from optimal and exhaustive search is impractical for real-time re-scheduling.

ALGORITHM DETAILS:
- Cost model: N-point real FFT cost = k*N*log2(N) madds (k = implementation-dependent constant, empirically ~1.5). Single-block overlap-save: O_FFT=2k log2(2N), O_SpecMult=4, O_FFT-1=2k log2(2N) => O_SB(N)=4k log2(2N)+4 madds/output-sample.
- Uniform multi-block partition (blocks of size N, T/N of them): O_MBUP(N) = 2k log2(2N)(1+T/N) + 5*T/N.
- Gardner (1995) non-uniform block-doubling: minimum-cost variant uses blocks N,N,2N,4N,...,2^(I-1)N (I=log2(T/N)); uniform-load variant uses N,N,2N,2N,4N,4N,...,2^(I-1)N,2^(I-1)N (doubled counts). Closed-form costs given as O_MBMC and O_MBUL (messy but exact closed forms derived in paper, both cheaper than MBUP but not FDL-optimal).
- Single-FDL (frequency-domain delay-line) convolution: all blocks size N, spectral-multiply-add results accumulated directly in the frequency domain across T/N branches (avoiding per-branch inverse FFTs), one inverse FFT total per output block. O_SingleFDL(N) = 4k log2(2N) + 4*(T/N). With P parallel channels mixed before the single inverse FFT: O_SingleFDL(N) = (1+1/P)*2k log2(2N) + 4*(T/N).
- Continuous relaxation for optimal single-FDL block size: dO/dN=0 gives N_opt = T*ln(2)/k; evaluate cost at the two nearest power-of-two candidates around N_opt and pick the minimum (since N must be power of two in practice).
- Double-FDL: header FDL fixed at block size N (dictated by latency spec), second FDL block size B free; O_DoubleFDL = 4k log2(2N) + 4*B/N + 4k log2(2B) + 4*(T/B - 1). Optimal B via same relax/derive/round procedure: B = -kN/[2ln2] + sqrt{(kN/[2ln2])^2 + TN}, then round to nearest power of two.
- Multiple-FDL optimal partition via Viterbi/DP: pointer moves over positions t=0..T/N-1 at n=t*N. State at position t = [S.Q]: S = current block's size in units of N, Q = which N-sample sub-fraction of that block. Legal-state/transition constraints: (1) block sizes must be powers of two; (2) a block of size S cannot begin before sample S of the IR; (3) block sizes along the IR must be non-decreasing. Transition cost rules: intra-block fraction advance [X.Q]->[X.Q+1] (Q<X) costs 0; closing a block and starting another block of the SAME size [X.X]->[X.1] costs one spectral-multiply-add = 4 madds; closing a block and starting a NEW/LARGER FDL [X.X]->[Y.1] (Y>X) costs a full direct FFT + spectral-multiply-add + inverse FFT = 4k log2(2YN)+4; with P parallel channels mixed in freq domain before a shared inverse FFT this drops to (2+2/P)k log2(2YN)+4; and specifically when Y=2X, reuse of previously-computed half-sized input spectra (per Ferrara 1980 split-spectrum trick) roughly halves the direct-FFT cost, giving (1+2/P)k log2(2YN)+4.
- Viterbi recursion: delta_t(i) = min_j[delta_{t-1}(j) + cost(j->i)]; Phi_t(i) = argmin_j[...]. Initialization: only legal state at t=0 is [1.1] (partition must start with a header block of size N to meet the latency spec), delta_0(0) = 4k log2(2N)+4. Termination/backtrack: i_{T/N-1} = argmin_i delta_{T/N-1}(i); then i_t = Phi_{t+1}(i_{t+1}) for t=T/N-2 downto 0, recovering the optimal block-size sequence (e.g. example trace [1.1][1.1][1.1][2.1][2.2][2.1][2.2][4.1][4.2][4.3][4.4][4.1][4.2][4.3][4.4] decodes to 3 FDLs: three N-blocks, two 2N-blocks, two 4N-blocks -- note the sequence is NOT monotonic in FDL occupancy per se, sub-fractions interleave, but block SIZE along the IR is non-decreasing).
- Complexity note: the DP replaces an exhaustive search over all legal partitions (paper cites 70,055,503 legal partitions for a realistic 3-second IR at 5.8ms latency) with a polynomial-time forward pass + backtrack, making it practical to re-run whenever IR length (or latency spec) changes -- directly applicable to a plugin needing to reschedule FDL partitions on-the-fly as IR 'size/stretch' parameters are modulated, though the paper itself only addresses static (per-IR-length) optimization, not real-time crossfaded re-partitioning between two different schedules (a gap the plugin design will need to solve separately, e.g. via dual-engine crossfade when the schedule itself changes).

Brandtsegg & Saue (DAFx-17) present "liveconv," a Csound opcode (and companion "Live convolver" VST) implementing click-free replacement of a time-variant impulse response (IR) inside a standard uniformly-partitioned, FFT-based (overlap-add) convolution engine — at zero extra computational cost versus static partitioned convolution. The core insight (their eq. 12, generalizing the direct-form convolution sum) is that convolution's inherent "fade-in/fade-out" ramp property (the summation in eq. 1 has 1 term at n=0, ramps to N terms at n=N-1, back to 1 term at n=2N-2) means a hard, one-coefficient-at-a-time replacement of an old IR hA(n) by a new IR hB(n), triggered at time nT and completed after N-1 further samples, produces a mathematically smooth transition identical in character to running two full overlapping time-invariant convolutions and cross-fading their outputs — but without the cost of a second convolution engine or an explicit crossfade multiply-add stage.

For partitioned convolution the replacement must be quantized to partition boundaries (nT = k·NP) and IR partitions are swapped in one at a time, starting from partition 0, in the same order the load/unload process already uses (justified by two proven properties of partitioned convolution: partition pair (i,j) only contributes to output block Y_k if i,j ≤ k, and only to Y_{P+k} if i,j > k — the ">" rather than "≥" bound is an OLA-specific correction the authors derive because half of each partition's output is buffered and added into the next block, so it needn't be recomputed). The transition spans exactly P-1 partitions (P = number of IR partitions), and during that window the output is a genuine mix of contributions from both hA and hB partitions still resident in the FFT-domain IR buffer — with no added multiply-accumulate cost because the total number of frequency-domain multiplications per step remains fixed (N-1, or one partition-FFT per partition-time). The same partition-by-partition load/unload mechanism (already needed just to get audio into the IR buffer in the first place) also enables (a) starting convolution before the IR is fully captured (latency = one partition instead of full IR length) and (b) spreading the FFT computation of a newly-captured IR over time instead of bursting it all at once.

They validated the method by comparing (i) two independent full-length time-invariant partitioned convolutions of a 10 s baroque-music input against two different 1 s, 44.1kHz speech IRs (hA, hB), merged at their crossover point nT≈4.5 s, versus (ii) a single time-variant "liveconv" run of the same input with stepwise IR replacement triggered at the same nT. The residual (difference signal) between the two outputs was "uniform and for all practical purposes negligible," giving a measured SNR of 67.2 dB — their headline empirical validation that stepwise partition replacement reproduces time-invariant convolution behavior indistinguishably. Test parameters: partition length NP = 1024, FFT block size NB = 2·NP = 2048, sample rate 44,100 Hz, IR length N = 44100 (1 s, P = N/NP ≈ 43 partitions).

Implementation is the Csound opcode `liveconv` (extending the existing fixed-IR opcode `ftconv`), constrained to power-of-two partition sizes (NP = 2^k) with FFT block NB = 2·NP (zero-padded per partition to avoid circular-convolution time-aliasing), using Overlap-Add (OLA) rather than Overlap-Save (they note OLS is more computationally efficient per Wefers 2015 but they leave OLS compatibility of the replacement scheme as future work). Per-audio-block (ksmps-rate) processing: check load/unload control signal → accumulate input into circular buffer → once a full partition of new input is buffered, take its FFT, take FFT of the IR partition(s) currently being loaded (multiple concurrent load/unload processes are supported, in principle one per partition), complex-multiply in frequency domain, inverse-FFT and OLA into output, and zero any partition currently being unloaded → advance all active load/unload process pointers. No fixed IR-length assumption is required at initialization beyond allocating the partition-length/table-size once; content can be updated freely at any time without a restart. A disadvantage explicitly noted: transition length is fixed to the full IR length N (not independently tunable, unlike a dedicated crossfade approach), and dynamic-range/amplitude control is not addressed by this scheme (called out as ongoing work). Their explicit contrast baseline is Wefers & Vorländer's 2014 Forum Acousticum frequency-domain crossfading method, which the authors note still needs an extra spectral convolution/inverse-transform pass that liveconv's coefficient-replacement approach avoids entirely.

The VST "Live convolver" plugin wraps liveconv in a dual-mono signal flow (left channel records into a circular IR buffer with optional time-reversal/pitch transforms before commit, right channel is the live convolution input), exposes IR update-rate/size, manual or transient-triggered IR capture, pitch shift, time reversal, HP/LP filtering, "brightening" high-shelf (to counter convolution's inherent HF energy loss when multiplying two naturally low-pass-leaning spectra), and a small-Hz frequency shifter (per Vila 2005, AES Convention 118) specifically to suppress feedback risk when the IR-recording mic and convolver output share a room. It has been used in live performances/studio sessions since late 2016 (with Kyle Motl at UCSD Feb 2017, and Jordan Morton, March 2017).

ALGORITHM DETAILS:
- Direct-form convolution with stepwise IR replacement (eq. 12): y_n = Σ_{k=max(0,n-nT+1)}^{N-1} x(n-k)hA(k) + Σ_{k=0}^{min(N-1,n-nT)} x(n-k)hB(k) — reduces to pure hA convolution for n<nT and pure hB convolution for n>nT+N-1; in between, hA coefficients are replaced one-by-one by hB coefficients starting from hA(0).
- Partitioned convolution setup: x(n) and h(n) each split into P uniform partitions of length NP=N/P (eqs 3-6); output contribution region of partition pair (xi,hj) spans intervals Y_{i+j} and Y_{i+j+1} where Y_k=[k·NP,(k+1)·NP-1] (eqs 8-10).
- Property 1: partition pair (i,j) contributes to output block Y_k only if i,j ≤ k → enables partition-by-partition progressive IR loading with reduced initial latency (down to one partition) and spread-out FFT compute.
- Property 2 (OLA-corrected): partition pair (i,j) contributes to output block Y_{P+k} only if i,j > k (not ≥, because for Overlap-Add half the output of interval Y_{i+j} is buffered and added into Y_{i+j+1}, so it needn't be recomputed) → enables partition-by-partition IR unloading (zeroing) without artifacts.
- IR replacement is triggered only at partition boundaries nT = k·NP (eq. 15); replacement proceeds by refilling the entire N-sample/P-partition IR buffer starting from partition 0 in the same order the load/unload logic already traverses partitions, ensuring no discontinuity within the IR itself.

- Per-processing-block algorithm: check load/unload control signal; accumulate audio into an internal circular buffer in ksmps-sized chunks; once a full partition of new input is filled, take its FFT; for each active load process, fetch and FFT the next needed IR partition (multiple concurrent load/unload processes supported, in principle up to one per partition); complex-multiply input and IR spectra; inverse-FFT and overlap-add into output; for each active unload process, zero the corresponding IR partition; advance/retire load-unload process state.

Core shared idea (confirmed for Wefers/Vorländer via Brandtsegg&Saue's direct paraphrase, and detailed via the Franck patent): the standard way to crossfade a time-varying FIR filter under fast (FFT-based/partitioned) convolution is to run TWO parallel fast-convolution chains (old filter H_A, new filter H_B), inverse-FFT each to the time domain, and crossfade the two time-domain outputs with a linear-ramp window. This costs a second IFFT (and the time-domain multiply-adds) purely for the transition. The Wefers/Vorländer (and, independently, Franck) insight is to instead multiply each filter's DFT-domain output spectrum by a small, sparsely-occupied frequency-domain "crossfade window" and sum the two windowed spectra in the frequency domain, THEN take a single inverse FFT. Because circularly convolving a spectrum by a window with only a handful of non-zero taps is cheap, this can undercut the cost of a second full IFFT, provided the window is kept sparse. Brandtsegg & Saue's own competing method (stepwise partition replacement, zero extra cost) explicitly positions itself against this DFT-domain crossfade approach as the two known alternatives to naive double-convolution-plus-time-domain-crossfade.

Algorithm mechanics (from the Franck patent, presented as the best-available stand-in for the missing primary source): Let X[m,k] be block-m DFT spectra of the (possibly partitioned) input, H1[k], H2[k] the two filter transfer functions (old/new, or two HRTFs), giving filtered spectra Y1[k]=X[k]·H1[k], Y2[k]=X[k]·H2[k]. Instead of IFFT(Y1) and IFFT(Y2) separately then time-domain crossfade y[n]=w1[n]y1[n]+w2[n]y2[n], the patent derives (via the DFT convolution-multiplication duality) that time-domain windowing by w[n] is equivalent to circular convolution of the spectrum with W[k]=DFT{w[n]}: Y[k] = Y1[k]⊛W1[k] + Y2[k]⊛W2[k], then a SINGLE IFFT of Y[k] gives the crossfaded time-domain output. For "constant-gain" crossfades (w1[n]+w2[n]=1, the normal HRTF/filter-exchange case) this reduces further to Y[k] = Y2[k] + s·Yd[k] + (e−s)·(W[k]⊛Yd[k]), where Yd[k]=Y1[k]−Y2[k] and s,e are the initial/final gain values of the ramp within the block — i.e. only ONE circular convolution is needed per crossfade, not two.

ALGORITHM DETAILS:
- Uniformly-partitioned convolution baseline (shared context, also in Brandtsegg&Saue): impulse response h[n] of length N split into P=⌈N/M⌉ partitions of M samples each, zero-padded to length L (standard choice L=2M, the 'standard DFT size'), giving DFT vectors H[p,k]=DFT{h_p[n]}; input segmented into overlapping blocks x[m,n] of length L with hop B between blocks; output Y[m,k]=Σ_{p=0}^{P-1} H[p,k]·X[m−p,k]; inverse DFT y[m,n]=IDFT{Y[m,k]}; only the last B samples of each output block are kept (time-domain aliasing avoided if M≤L−B+1).
- Wefers/Vorländer's stated two options for time-varying FIR filtering per Brandtsegg&Saue's citation: (a) instantaneous switching y_n = x*h_A for n<n_T, x*h_B for n≥n_T — cheap but produces audible click artifacts from waveform discontinuity; (b) crossfading — filter with both h_A and h_B over a transition and crossfade the two outputs, which (assuming frequency-domain fast convolution) costs 'an extra spectral convolution and inverse transform, as well as the operations consumed by the crossfade.' Their DFT-domain variant 'saves the extra inverse transform.'
- DFT-domain crossfade derivation (Franck patent, eq 15-16): time-domain windowing y[n]=x[n]·w[n] is dual, via the DFT convolution theorem, to a circular convolution in frequency: DFT{x[n]·w[n]} = (1/L)·DFT{x[n]} ⊛ DFT{w[n]}, so multiplying by a time window is equivalent to circularly convolving the spectrum X[k] with the window's spectrum W[k].
- Sparse frequency-domain window trick (core efficiency claim): a full circular convolution of two length-L sequences costs O(L^2) complex multiply-adds — worse than the FFT/IFFT it's meant to replace. But if W[k] is sparsely occupied (only K non-zero coefficients, K≪L), the circular convolution sum Y[k]=ΣW[l]X[(k−l) mod L] collapses to only K terms per output bin, becoming cheaper than an extra IFFT. Design constraint used: only the last B samples of the L-length time-domain window w[n] actually matter (due to the overlap-save mechanism), so the leading L−B coefficients of the corresponding ideal time-domain window are free/unconstrained — extra degrees of freedom exploited to find very-sparse W[k].
- Window design method: pose as constrained optimization — minimize ||G·W − ŵ||_p over the non-zero coefficient vector W, where ŵ is the target (last-B-samples) time-domain window and G is a matrix of basis cosine/sine functions (real coefficients only affect even bins, imaginary only odd bins, by conjugate-symmetry of a real window's spectrum); p=2 for least-squares or p=∞ for Chebyshev/minimax design; solved with the CVX convex-optimization package. Index sets R (which bins get real coefficients) and I (which get imaginary) are free design choices; empirically the best K-coefficient sets use bin 0 and odd bins for real values, even bins (from 2) for imaginary values.
- Concrete window-design numeric results (Franck patent, for B=64, L=2B=128, approximating an ideal linear-ramp crossfade window): K=15 (R={0..7} real, I={1..7} imag) gives L2 error 9.372e-6, L∞ error 5.653e-6 (near-exact); K=8 with all-real coefficients (R={0..7}, I={}) gives markedly worse L2=5.452e-2, L∞=1.55e-2; K=8 with the optimal real/imag split (R={0,1,3,5,7}, I={2,4,6}) nearly matches the K=15 result: L2=9.388e-6, L∞=7.102e-6; even K=3 (R={0,1}, I={2}) gives usable L2=4.676e-2; K=2 (R={0,1}, no imaginary) gives L2=5.403e-1 (poor but 'may be acceptable... for a smooth transition with no signal discontinuities'). General recommendation: keep window coefficient count ≤18, more advantageously ≤15, ≤8, ≤4, ≤3, or as low as 2.
- Complexity formulas (Franck patent, section 'Complexity Evaluation'): sparse circular convolution with K non-zero window coefficients costs 4K⌈(L+1)/2⌉ real multiplications + 2(K−1)⌈(L+1)/2⌉ real additions (exploiting conjugate symmetry of real signals/filters, so only ⌈(L+1)/2⌉ of L bins need be computed). For constant-gain crossfading specifically (only one circular convolution needed instead of two, per Yd[k] trick), total cost per block of B output samples = (10K−2)⌈(L+1)/2⌉ + 2K + 1 real-valued instructions.
- Benchmark comparison numbers (Franck patent, Fig. 8, baseline config N=512 filter length, B=128 block size, L=256 standard DFT size, M=8 virtual sound sources, K=4 non-zero window coefficients): (a) partitioned convolution + time-domain crossfading (2 IFFTs) vs (b) DFT-domain crossfading per source (1 IFFT per source, this patent's method) vs (c) DFT-domain crossfading with the summation of multiple sources also done in the frequency domain before a single shared final IFFT. For N=512, single channel: complexity falls from ≈186 to ≈131 instructions per output sample (method b vs a). With multiple sources summed in the frequency domain (method c), gains are much larger — for M=2..8 virtual sources 'considerable gains in efficiency' are shown, with only small additional gains for larger scenes (asymptotic limit set by the forward FFT/block-convolution cost, which cannot be reduced further by cutting IFFTs). For window sizes up to ~7 non-zero components in a per-channel implementation, or up to ~12 non-zero components when channel summation is also done in the frequency domain, DFT-domain crossfading beats time-domain crossfading.
- Typical filter/application scale cited: HRTF filter lengths are 'roughly 200–1000' taps, for which uniformly-partitioned convolution is usually most efficient; the technique is proposed for binaural synthesis (crossfading between interpolated HRTFs as a sound source moves) and for beamforming (crossfading loudspeaker/microphone-array steering filters) — i.e. head/steering-vector time-variation, not literally reverb IR morphing, but directly transferable to a convolution-reverb engine's IR-parameter-crossfade problem.

This engineering report introduces TVOLAP (Time-Variant Overlap-Add in Partitions), a partitioned frequency-domain convolution algorithm designed specifically to let the transfer function (impulse response) be swapped during real-time playback without audible click/glitch artifacts, while keeping computational load constant (no spikes at switch time) and decoupling audio latency / switching latency from IR length via partitioning. It positions TVOLAP against four baselines — direct time-domain convolution, OLA, OLS, WOLA, and CF-TDC (cross-faded time-domain convolution, i.e. running two convolvers and crossfading) — with exact FLOPS, audio-latency and switching-latency numbers for NIR=2048 and NIR=512 examples at fs=48kHz, plus qualitative/visual comparisons using real KEMAR HRIR (TU Berlin) and BRIR (Salford-BBC SBSBRIR) data showing OLA/OLS produce hard clicks on IR switch, WOLA produces a smooth but slow crossfade tied to full block size, and TVOLAP produces a smooth crossfade at 1/M the latency of WOLA for the same computational cost.

Algorithmically: input signal is split into 50%-overlapping blocks of length 2L (hop L), Hann-windowed (periodic Hann, eq. 2). IR is split into M non-overlapping partitions of length 2L (rectangular window). Each is zero-padded to 4L and FFT'd (double-length zero padding to avoid circular-convolution wraparound, a la standard overlap-save/partitioned-convolution zero-padding). Intermediate spectra per block ℓ are formed as Y(k,ℓ) = Σ_{m=0}^{M-1} H(k,m)·X(k,ℓ−2m) — note the input block index decrements by 2 per partition step (because input hop is L but partition length is 2L, so consecutive partitions reference blocks two hops apart). After inverse FFT (length 4L, giving y(n',ℓ) of length 4L), a two-stage overlap-add reconstructs output: stage 1 sums the right half (samples 2L..4L-1) of the previous block's IFFT output with the left half (samples 0..2L-1) of the current block's IFFT output to form a length-2L block ŷ(n',ℓ); stage 2 overlap-adds these length-2L blocks shifted by hop L to produce final output ỹ(n). Time-variance is achieved trivially: at a switch instant, the H(k,m) partitions are swapped for new P(k,m) partitions used in the very next multiply-accumulate; the paper states "all transfer function partitions must be exchanged at the same time" — i.e., switching is atomic across all M partitions, not gradual per-partition.

Key efficiency argument: TVOLAP behaves like WOLA (Hann-windowed, 50% overlap-add) but with a rectangular synthesis window and a partitioned IR, so it decouples the audio latency and switching latency (both tied to block size 2L) from the total IR length NIR (tied to M·2L). This is the mechanism the plugin design would exploit: pick a small 2L for low latency/fast switching and just raise M for longer reverb tails, at a computational cost that grows roughly linearly with M (dominated by the M complex multiply-accumulates) rather than by re-running whole-IR-length FFTs, and — crucially — with NO added compute or latency spike at the moment of IR switching, unlike CF-TDC which doubles compute during crossfade windows.

ALGORITHM DETAILS:
- Input windowing: block length 2L, hop L (50% overlap), periodic Hann window w_hann(n) = 1 − cos(2πn/2L) for 0≤n<2L applied to blocks x̂(n−ℓL, ℓ) = x̃(n)·w_hann(n−ℓL).
- IR partitioning: split h̃(n) into M non-overlapping partitions of length 2L using a rectangular window w_rect, indexed by m: ĥ(n−m·2L, m) = h̃(n)·w_rect(n−m·2L).
- Each windowed input block and each IR partition is zero-padded by 2L zeros (to total 4L) before FFT — 'double length zero padding' — giving FFT length 4L for both X(k,ℓ) and H(k,m).
- Spectral multiply-accumulate: Y(k,ℓ) = Σ_{m=0}^{M−1} H(k,m)·X(k, ℓ−2m) (note: input block index decremented by 2 per partition index m, because IR partitions are length 2L = 2 input hops).
- Time-domain intermediate signal: y(n',ℓ) = IDFT_{4L}(Y(k,ℓ)), length 4L per block.
- Two-step overlap-add reconstruction: (1) ŷ(n',ℓ) = y_PR(n',ℓ) + y_CL(n',ℓ) where y_PR(n',ℓ)=y(n'+2L, ℓ−1) (right half of previous intermediate block) and y_CL(n',ℓ)=y(n',ℓ) (left half of current block), for 0≤n'<2L; (2) final output ỹ(n) = Σ_ℓ ŷ(n−ℓL, ℓ), i.e. these length-2L blocks are overlap-added at hop L.
- Time-variant IR switching mechanism: swap H(k,m) → P(k,m) (all M partitions simultaneously) in the multiply-accumulate step (eq. 8) — no special crossfade logic needed; the existing Hann-windowed OLA machinery naturally produces a smooth transition because the swap only affects future multiply-accumulates while the overlap-add of previous already-computed blocks continues to settle.
- Baseline algorithm definitions for comparison: OLA = Allen & Rabiner 1977 block FFT convolution (block size = NIR, no overlap, no window) — time-invariant only, hard-switch artifacts. OLS = Overlap-Save variant, discards circular-convolution-corrupted spectrum portion — also produces hard/click switches but no distortion tail (since it doesn't reuse stale partial outputs). WOLA = Crochiere 1980 weighted OLA: 50% overlapping analysis+synthesis Hann/sqrt-Hann windows, block size = NIR (not partitioned) — smooth crossfade but switching latency tied to full NIR. CF-TDC = two independent full time-domain convolutions run in parallel, continuously, with their outputs crossfaded — used as the 'ideal but expensive' reference for switching quality.
- FLOPS derivation for OLA: real FFT of length 2·NIR via decimation-in-time needs (NIR/2)·log2(NIR) butterflies + overhead of 4·NIR multiplications + 10·NIR additions; one butterfly = 4 real multiplies + 6 real adds; total per FFT = 10·(NIR/2)·log2(NIR) + 14·NIR ops, i.e. 5·log2(NIR)+14 ops/sample (same for inverse). OLA total = FFT+IFFT + NIR(+1) complex multiplies (6 ops each) + NIR additions per block = 2·(5·log2(NIR)+14) + 6 + 1 = 145 ops/sample for NIR=2048 → 145·48000 = 6.96 MFLOPS.
- WOLA FLOPS: same per-sample cost as OLA but doubled because of 50% overlap (each sample transformed/multiplied/added twice) plus 2 extra multiplies + 1 extra add per sample for window application: 2·(145+3)·48000 = 14.064 MFLOPS for NIR=2048 (~2x OLA cost).
- TVOLAP FLOPS (2L=512, M=4, NIR=2048): (20·log2(512) + 56 + 64 + 4)·48000 = 14.592 MFLOPS — comparable to WOLA cost but with 1/4 the audio and switching latency; general formula components: 2·2·(5·log2(2L)+14) for forward+inverse transform overhead (factor 2 for 50% overlap × factor 2 for transform+inverse), plus 2M complex multiply-adds (≈8 ops each) for the M-partition MAC, plus 2·2 multiplies for input windowing and output overlap-add summation.
- Direct time-domain convolution FLOPS: 2·N·fs per second (N multiplies + N adds per output sample) → for N=2048, fs=48000: 2·2048·48000 = 196.608 MFLOPS.
- CF-TDC extra cost: doubles direct-convolution FLOPS during any crossfade plus 3·fs extra MFLOPS (3 extra multiplies+adds per sample) for the crossfade mixing itself, e.g. 0.144 extra MFLOPS at fs=48kHz — switching latency is user-selectable (tied to crossfade length) but comes with zero added audio latency, at the cost of continuously running two full convolvers.
